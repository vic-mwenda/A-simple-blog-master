What misses in Jubilee self
assessment

july21.3 min read

The Jubilee administration has done a self appraisal and held press conferences to make this public, a smart public engagement move if you ask me. Self assessment is a practice in modern management and has been adopted by corporate organizations for some time now. It takes boldness to not only talk about your successes but also admit your shortcomings. The self assessments press conferences takes this to the next level: engaging with the public. Uhuruto The briefings however exposed something on public engagements that governments and organizations need to appreciate: the power of linking events and performance to impact. Events don’t mean so much to people unless they have an impact on them. It is even more important in governance where perception is everything. Government must approach this from the angle that over time public trust on the governments has waned owing to empty talk by politicians. The goal should therefore be to prove the achievements have impacted on the public and not just parade them. Saying we established free maternity for example isn’t showing an achievement, indicating how many mothers and babies this has saved is. Saying we have leased over 1,000 police vehicles isn’t showing an achievement, showing how that has helped improve security is. Showing figures goes a long way in proving the impact. The National Treasury Secretary for example said: “We have worked closely with KRA to combat any tax evasion.” He would have done better by giving statistics of how much has been saved from this. The Foreign Affairs Ministry for example said: In the last one year we have been able to conclude bilateral trade agreements with different countries. One question the man in the street would ask is so what? Bilateral agreements are good but until you show us their impact, it does not make sense. For example the same day I saw that a Kenyan company MEA Ltd was to establish a fertilizer factory in Ethiopia while Brookside is engaging business in Ethiopia and Nigeria. These is after the facilitation of the agreements. The ministry would have used it to show impact. Example of statements that showed impact include one by Foreign affairs Principle Secretary: “The Ministry has negotiated contentious issues in EPA from 9 to current 3.” The Transport PS indicated that new regulations had reduced accidents by about 25%. He could have stopped at the point of having new regulations, but by indicating their impact it shows a Kenyan what the new regulations have done. I am convinced that the Jubilee government has achieved great milestones, but they could do much better in their communication. At the end of the day shouting your good deeds is good, but it is smarter to show how they relate to those you serve.
